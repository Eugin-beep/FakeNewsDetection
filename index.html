What to look for

¶
Context mismatchDoes the caption's claimed date and location match what the image actually shows? Old disaster or protest photos are frequently relabeled as "breaking."
¶
Signs of AI generationCheck hands, text in the background, and lighting consistency — common giveaways in AI-generated images. No automated check is fully reliable yet, so treat this as a prompt to look closer, not a verdict.
¶
Video provenanceRight-click a video frame and reverse-search a screenshot; check if the same clip appears attached to older, unrelated stories.

Problem statement

Fake News Detection

Misinformation now spreads fastest through emotionally charged headlines, disguised publishers, out-of-context media, and closed messaging apps — often faster than professional fact-checkers can respond. TruthLens combines automated screening with transparent reasoning and human verification habits, rather than a single black-box verdict.

Responsible AI guardrails

¶
Confidence, not certaintyEvery result shows a confidence level and the specific signals behind it — never a bare true/false verdict.
¶
Human appeal pathThe "Flag this result as wrong" button represents a required override channel for any automated flag — no user should be stuck with a verdict they can't contest.
¶
Bias auditingBecause misinformation exists across the political spectrum, flagging logic must be regularly audited for skew and published transparently.
¶
Assistive, not authoritativeTruthLens is designed to support a reader's own judgment and media-literacy skills — not to replace professional fact-checking or be treated as a final ruling.

How this build works

This is a client-side prototype. The linguistic analyzer, lookalike-domain checker, and quiz run real logic in your browser. The source-credibility lookup and cross-reference check use a small illustrative dataset bundled in the code (see the DATA section in the script) standing in for the live APIs listed in the About tab's roadmap table.

Educational prototype, not a certified fact-checking service. A low score is a prompt to verify further — not proof something is false. A high score is not a guarantee of accuracy. Always cross-check with a trusted, independent source before sharing.
